WMEN CHIP

Minorities bore the burden of the COVID-19 pandemic's mental toll. We hope to illuminate trends to draw light to how women have suffered.


WMN Chip is a new initiative to provide data visualizations about the mental health impact of the covid pandemic collected from 2020-2021 based on gender.

Average Perceived Stress Score for Participants by Gender



In this survey, participants' stress was measured using the Perceived Stress Scale, which asks 10 questions about stress. Ratings for each questions were done on a scale from 1-5, where 5 generally corresponded to more stress i.e. a worsened mental state. The chart plots the average PSS score on the x-axis against the amount of people who had that score on the y-axis. On the chart, women visibly make up most of the higher scores, compared to the middle scores where women and men make up an equal amount of the score. This may indicates that on average, the women in this survey described being more stressed than the men.

Average Perceived Loneliness Score for Participants by Gender

In this survey, participants' loneliness was measured using the UCLA 3 Item Loneliness Scale, which asks 3 questions about loneliness. Ratings for each questions were done on a scale from 1-5, where 5 corresponded to very lonely i.e. a worsened mental state. The chart plots the average loneliness score on the x-axis against the amount of people who had that score on the y-axis. Interestingly, women seem to make up most of the high loneliness scores (i.e. scores from the 4.33 - 5 range) compared to men.

Participants' Level of Trust in People by Gender


In this survey, participants' self-reported level of social support was measured. The survey is the Social Provisions Survey. Ratings for 10 questions were done on a scale from strongly disagree (1) to strongly agree (6), where strongly agree corresponded to high level of social support. The chart plots participants' average self-reported level of social support on the x-axis against the amount of people who had that score on the y-axis. Women seem to make up most of the high support scores, suggesting that encouraging women to check in with loved ones may be an effective resource at reducing high levels of stress and loneliness among women during the pandemic.

So, what should we do?

  • Even if you are isolated at home, try to keep to a regular schedule as much as possible. While loneliness can feel like it will never end, trying to make these days feel as "normal" as possible will help you to get through. Start each day with a plan of a few things that you will do, keep a daily diary about how you are feeling and what you are doing, and keep a symptom log if you are managing illness.
